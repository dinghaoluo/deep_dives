# Session 2 — 12 Apr 2026

**Aperture:** In 1906, a German general issued an order that historians would later call the first genocide of the twentieth century. He was recalled to Berlin shortly after. He was then celebrated.
**Thread ended:** The legibility problem has no resolution — only a discipline of asking, whenever you *can* perceive something, what structurally similar thing you are not seeing.

---

## The Thread

The session opened on Lothar von Trotha and moved quickly to why the German colonial project attracted *scientists* rather than just soldiers — arriving at Eugen Fischer's fieldwork on the Rehoboth Basters as the founding moment of Rassenhygiene, with a direct personnel and methodological line to Nazi medical experimentation. The key analytical move was distinguishing *colonial pre-anonymisation* from ordinary hierarchy: Fischer didn't have to overcome resistance to objectification because the colonial apparatus had already stripped legal personhood before he arrived, creating a vacancy the science simply slotted into. This led to Pynchon's structural argument in *Gravity's Rainbow* — that the V-2 didn't invent the abstraction of human targets, it brought the colonial epistemology home and pointed it westward, the rocket operator firing a calculation at a coordinate. The Congo/Namibia asymmetry then revealed how atrocity-visibility is a political product: Leopold was attackable without attacking empire as such, whereas Germany was a great power and Casement was dispatched partly because embarrassing Leopold cost Britain nothing. Samuel Maharero's letters — formally precise, in exactly the register power could read — went unanswered. The session closed on Singer's drowning child, Dinghao's 2022 Chinese piece on abstract vs. concrete people, and the argument that the legibility problem's only honest answer is a discipline, not a resolution.

## Key Nodes

- **Lothar von Trotha (1848–1920):** Issued the *Vernichtungsbefehl* in German Southwest Africa, 1906; recalled, officially reprimanded, then celebrated. The order was rescinded on paper; the camps continued under colonial bureaucracy.
- **Eugen Fischer (1874–1967):** Conducted fieldwork on mixed-race Rehoboth Basters in Namibia; his 1913 book became foundational to Nazi racial science. Hitler read it in Landsberg. The line from Fischer to Mengele is institutional and personnel, not merely metaphorical.
- **Colonial pre-anonymisation:** The colonial apparatus renders populations scientifically anonymous *before* the scientist arrives — not overcoming resistance to objectification but filling a vacancy empire created. Distinct from online anonymity (absence of information) vs. organised erasure of legal personhood.
- **The V-2 as colonial epistemology brought home:** Pynchon's argument in *GR* — Blicero/Weissmann is a colonial figure before a rocket figure. The V-2 was the first weapon system designed to kill people its operators would never see, identify, or have any relationship with: target as coordinate, category, pre-anonymised.
- **Congo vs. Namibia asymmetry:** Congo got Casement because Leopold was uniquely attackable — a small king running a personal fiefdom. Namibia got nothing. The *Vernichtungsbefehl* was officially rescinded; killing continued as administrative logistics, invisible as genocide because it looked like the aftermath of war.
- **Roger Casement (1864–1916):** British consul who invented the human rights investigation — dry, precise, consular in tone, devastating because it read like evidence not sentiment. Congo 1904, Amazon 1910. Effectiveness came from form, not fervour.
- **Samuel Maharero (c.1856–1923):** Herero paramount chief who escaped to British Bechuanaland and spent years petitioning the British government to intervene. His letters survive — formally precise, in the register power could read. Britain did not act.
- **August Bebel (1840–1913):** German Social Democrat who named the *Vernichtungsbefehl* for what it was in the Reichstag. Largely ignored.
- **Peter Singer, 'Famine, Affluence, and Morality' (1972):** The drowning child argument — proximity should not determine moral weight. Distance attenuates response as a fact of psychology, not moral reality.
- **Dinghao's 2022 piece (神经现实):** 1914 Christmas truce + Dark Souls PvP ritual as parallel instances of re-individuation against systemic de-individuation. Abstract 'them' cannot be changed; concrete 'him/her/thon' can.

## Dinghao's Best Contributions

- On why the colony enabled objectification: intuited that colonial hierarchy had already performed the work of objectification before Fischer arrived. The sharper formulation is that the colony performed a prior *epistemological* act, not just a physical one.
- On form vs. intent as moral anchors: intent is historically contingent (Fischer thought he was doing moral science; American eugenicists thought they were improving humanity), therefore unreliable; dignity-violation is more stable and legible. Carefully structured; productively pushed on.
- On karma as resolution: identified it honestly as non-resolutive — "karma did not do shit for the murdered natives." Named it as a psychological shield rather than a philosophical answer.
- On the internet analogy to colonial illegibility: proposed online anonymity as a similar illegibility problem. Accepted pushback that the mechanics differ — absence of information vs. organised erasure — and the distinction held.
